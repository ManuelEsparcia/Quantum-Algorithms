Cut a graph in two.

A simple-looking problem. You're given a graph — vertices connected by edges. Your job is to colour each vertex one of two colours, say teal and amber, so that the number of edges connecting different colours is as large as possible. That's MaxCut, and despite its plain statement it's NP-hard. No classical algorithm runs in polynomial time on every instance unless P=NP.

Why it's hard

Exponential search.

For n vertices there are 2ⁿ ways to partition them. For 100 vertices that's already 10³⁰ — more than every grain of sand on Earth.

The best classical algorithm (Goemans–Williamson, 1995) doesn't find the optimum — it guarantees a cut of at least ≈ 87.8% of the maximum, in polynomial time. Doing better is also believed to be NP-hard.

QAOA is a quantum algorithm that attacks problems like this one. It doesn't promise to find the exact optimum either — it's approximate by design. But the hope is that on real hardware it might find solutions that beat what the best classical methods can produce, especially on instances that classical heuristics struggle with.

The classical landscape.

There are several ways to attack MaxCut classically. None of them runs in polynomial time and finds the exact optimum (because the problem is NP-hard) — they trade off between speed and quality. Here's the menu, with what each method delivers.

Method 1 · Brute force

Enumerate all 2ⁿ partitions, score each, keep the best. Exact but exponential. Doable up to maybe n ≈ 30 on a laptop. Past that, infeasible.

Method 2 · Greedy heuristic

Start with a random partition, flip whichever single vertex improves the cut the most, repeat until no flip helps. Polynomial, often fast — but gets stuck in local optima far from the true maximum.

Method 3 · Goemans–Williamson

The state-of-the-art classical approximation (1995, Gödel Prize 2012). Uses semidefinite programming to round a continuous relaxation. Guarantees a cut of at least 87.8% of the optimum, in polynomial time. Doing better is also believed to be NP-hard.

Below: an interactive comparison of brute-force vs greedy vs random partitioning on the 5-vertex graph from the previous section. Brute-force always wins; greedy usually gets close but can plateau. QAOA's challenge is to do at least as well as greedy, and ideally as well as Goemans–Williamson, on graph families where classical methods struggle.

Depth vs quality.

QAOA has one main knob: p, the number of alternating layers. More layers means a deeper circuit, more parameters to optimize, and (usually) better solutions. Each layer adds 2 parameters: one phase angle γ and one mixing angle β. The expressive power grows with p, but so does the optimization difficulty and the noise sensitivity on real hardware.

Encode, phase, mix, repeat.

QAOA's recipe is short. There are three ingredients and one rhythm: alternating two unitaries to gradually concentrate amplitude on good solutions.

Ingredient 1 — encode the problem. Each partition of n vertices is a bitstring in {0,1}ⁿ, so each partition corresponds to a basis state of an n-qubit register. For each edge (i,j) in the graph, define a "cost" term that's +1 when both endpoints have the same colour (edge not cut) and 0 when they differ (edge cut). Sum over all edges and you have the cost Hamiltonian:

C = ½ Σ(i,j) ∈ E (1 − Zi Zj)
Each computational basis state |x⟩ is an eigenvector of C with eigenvalue equal to the cut size of that partition. So maximizing ⟨C⟩ is the same as MaxCut.

Ingredient 2 — a "mixer". The mixer Hamiltonian is the simplest possible: a sum of X on each qubit. It has no preferred direction in the computational basis — it just spreads amplitude around between basis states.

B = Σi Xi
Each Xi flips qubit i. The unitary e^(−iβB) rotates each qubit by angle 2β around the X axis — a uniform mixing.

Ingredient 3 — alternate. Start in the uniform superposition over all partitions. Apply e^(−iγ₁C) to imprint relative phases proportional to the cut sizes, then e^(−iβ₁B) to mix the resulting interference pattern. Repeat p times with new γₖ, βₖ each round. Tune the 2p parameters with a classical optimizer to maximize ⟨C⟩. Measure the final state and read off a partition.

|ψ(γ, β)⟩ = e^(−iβₚB) e^(−iγₚC) ⋯ e^(−iβ₁B) e^(−iγ₁C) |+⟩⊗n
The deeper the circuit (larger p), the closer this gets to the adiabatic evolution that would, in the limit, give the exact optimum.

Watch what happens as you increase p: more parameters means a more expressive ansatz, and the optimizer can find a better expected cut. But with more parameters the optimization landscape gets harder — random initial guesses get stuck more often, and gradient descent can plateau. This is the central tension in QAOA practice. There's no convergence theorem like the variational principle for VQE; QAOA's quality depends on the choice of mixer, the graph structure, and the optimizer's luck.

Optimization, on quantum hardware.

QAOA is VQE's twin: same hybrid loop, same sensitivity to ansatz design and optimizer behaviour, but aimed at combinatorial problems instead of physical eigenvalues. Anywhere you can write down a cost function over bitstrings — MaxCut, graph colouring, MaxSAT, portfolio optimization, vehicle routing — QAOA gives you a recipe to attack it.

The honest assessment as of writing: QAOA has not yet beaten classical algorithms on any benchmark of practical interest. Real hardware is small, noisy, and the optimization landscapes are hard. But it remains one of the most-studied quantum algorithms specifically because it requires only modest depth, fits the NISQ regime, and offers a clean theoretical bridge to the quantum adiabatic algorithm in the limit p → ∞. Whether it ends up giving a real-world quantum advantage is one of the open questions of the field.
