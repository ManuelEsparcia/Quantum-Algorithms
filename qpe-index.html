Quantum Phase Estimation

Reading the eigenphase of a unitary operator — the missing link between the QFT and Shor's factoring.

A black box with a hidden phase.

A new kind of box. This one takes a quantum state |ψ⟩ as input and applies a unitary operator U. You have a particular guarantee: |ψ⟩ is an eigenvector of U, which means U|ψ⟩ = e^(2πi·φ)|ψ⟩. The output state is the same as the input — except the amplitude has been multiplied by a complex number of magnitude 1, with phase 2π·φ. Your job is to find φ.

What U does

The phase rotation.

For an eigenvector |ψ⟩, the box returns |ψ⟩ back unchanged in direction — but multiplied by e^(2πi·φ). The number φ ∈ [0, 1) is the eigenphase you want to read.

Why it matters

The hidden engine.

Read out the eigenphase of a modular-multiplication operator and you have the period of a^x mod N — the heart of Shor's factoring. Read out the eigenphase of a Hamiltonian's evolution operator and you have its energy levels — quantum chemistry in one trick.

Why "phase"? Every unitary has eigenvalues of magnitude exactly 1. Each one can be written as e^(2πi·φ) for some φ. Phase estimation reads that φ, to as many bits of precision as you want.

The catch. You can't just measure the phase — global phases are invisible to a measurement of |ψ⟩ alone. Worse, if you start with a generic state instead of an eigenvector, U acts on each eigencomponent with a different phase, and any single measurement just gives back a basis state with no phase information at all. Phase estimation is a cleverness about how to make a phase measurable.

Why classical doesn't work.

Phase estimation is the first algorithm in this series with no classical analogue. The reason is mechanical: the eigenphase φ never appears in any direct observation of |ψ⟩. Apply U, the state rotates by e^(2πi·φ), but a measurement of |ψ⟩ in any basis is statistically identical to a measurement of |ψ⟩ before the rotation. Global phases cancel out of probabilities. The phase is genuinely hidden from any single-state experiment.

Why a phase isn't directly measurable

A measurement in any basis returns a basis state with probability equal to the squared magnitude of the amplitude. If two amplitudes differ only by a global phase factor e^(2πi·φ), their squared magnitudes are identical: |e^(2πi·φ)|² = 1. The phase factor cancels out. No matter how many copies of U|ψ⟩ you measure, you can't tell the difference between the rotated and unrotated state.

The trick of phase estimation is to not measure |ψ⟩. Instead, set up a second register of qubits whose Hadamard structure entangles with the phase, then read out that. The phase rotation gets imprinted on a register where it can be observed.

There are classical algorithms that can find eigenphases of a unitary if you have an explicit matrix representation — diagonalization, basically. But that costs O(N³) arithmetic operations on an N×N matrix, and modern interesting unitaries have N = 2ⁿ for hundreds of qubits — astronomically beyond reach. QPE works directly with the unitary as a black box that can be applied, never computing its matrix form.

The cost of precision.

Phase estimation reads t bits of φ using t control qubits and 2ᵗ − 1 applications of U. That sounds expensive — but each "application of U" can itself be a polynomial-time quantum routine, and the alternative classically is to diagonalize a 2ⁿ × 2ⁿ matrix, which is utterly infeasible. The exponential growth in U-applications is on a vastly smaller base.

Phase kickback, then Fourier.

The mechanism of QPE has two ingredients you've already met. The first is phase kickback: when you control a unitary U by a qubit in superposition, the phase that U would impose on its target gets transferred to the control qubit instead. The second is the QFT⁻¹: the inverse Fourier transform turns a phase-encoded state back into a peaked distribution over basis states.

Combine them like this. Take a register of t control qubits, all in |+⟩ via Hadamard. For each control qubit k, apply U^(2ᵏ) conditioned on it. Phase kickback puts a phase of e^(2πi · 2ᵏ · φ) on the |1⟩ branch of qubit k. Across all t control qubits, the resulting amplitudes are exactly the Fourier transform of the basis state |⌊2ᵗ · φ⌉⟩. Apply QFT⁻¹ to the control register, measure, read the answer.

QPE: t Hadamards · controlled-U^(2ᵏ) · QFT⁻¹ · measure
Each control qubit picks up its own slice of φ, encoded as a phase. The QFT⁻¹ converts those phases into a peak.

The intuition: the 2ᵏ in U^(2ᵏ) is exactly the right ratio to encode the binary expansion of φ. Qubit k ends up with phase 2π · 2ᵏ · φ, which depends on bits of φ at position k. The full QFT⁻¹ reads them all out at once.

Below: a single qubit demonstration of phase kickback. The control qubit starts in |+⟩. Apply controlled-U with φ = 0.5 and the control qubit becomes |−⟩ — the phase has jumped from the (target) eigenvector to the control. Toggle φ to see how the angle of the control's amplitude tracks the phase.

The phase has been kicked from the eigenvector to the control register, where you can actually observe it. Now scale this up to t control qubits, each with its own power of U, and you get a Fourier-encoded version of φ — which the inverse QFT decodes into the answer.

When φ is exactly representable in t bits — like φ = 1/2 = 0.1₂ for t = 3 — the final measurement returns the answer with probability 1. When φ isn't a clean dyadic fraction, the distribution concentrates around the nearest k/2ᵗ, with high probability of getting the right answer rounded to t bits.

Two patterns to look for. First, the more control qubits you use, the sharper the peak — each extra qubit doubles the resolution. Second, when φ is exactly k/2ᵗ, the peak is a single delta with probability 1. When φ falls between dyadic fractions, the probability mass spreads across several adjacent outcomes, with the highest concentration on the nearest k/2ᵗ.

One step away from Shor.

Phase estimation is the last building block before Shor's factoring algorithm. To factor a composite number N, Shor's procedure picks a random a coprime to N, then uses QPE to find the order of a modulo N — the smallest r with aʳ ≡ 1 (mod N). The unitary in question is "multiply by a mod N"; its eigenphases turn out to be multiples of 1/r; QPE reads them and a continued-fraction expansion recovers r. From there, simple classical arithmetic yields a factor of N.

Phase estimation is also the workhorse of quantum chemistry algorithms (eigenvalues of molecular Hamiltonians) and HHL-style linear-systems solvers. Anywhere you need to read out a unitary's spectrum, QPE is the tool.
