Quantum Fourier Transform

The engine that drives quantum phase estimation and Shor's factoring — and the cleanest example of quantum interference doing real work.

Frequencies, written in amplitudes.

A new tool, and a different kind of payoff. The Quantum Fourier Transform takes a quantum state and rewrites it in the basis of frequencies. A periodic structure in the input becomes a sharp peak in the output. A flat input becomes a single delta. The QFT doesn't solve a problem on its own — it's the workhorse that exposes hidden periodicity, which is exactly what the next two algorithms (Phase Estimation and Shor's factoring) will exploit.

The classical version

The DFT.

The discrete Fourier transform takes N numbers and returns N coefficients, one per frequency. A periodic signal of period r produces large coefficients at multiples of N/r.

The quantum version

The QFT.

The same map, applied to the amplitudes of a quantum state. The transform itself takes only O(log² N) gates — exponentially fewer than the classical FFT's O(N log N) arithmetic operations.

There's a catch — the quantum advantage doesn't extract the full coefficient list. A single measurement returns only one frequency, sampled with probability proportional to the squared amplitude. But for problems where one sharp peak is what you're looking for, that's exactly enough.

How to read this

The signal is a real function on 16 points — each bar represents one input value. The spectrum shows the magnitude of each of the 16 Fourier coefficients. A periodic signal of period r shows up as a peak at frequency N/r. A constant signal becomes a single peak at frequency 0. A delta becomes a flat spectrum.

The same mathematical transform applies to quantum amplitudes — that's the QFT. The two big differences are: the QFT can be computed in O(log² N) gates instead of O(N log N) arithmetic, and you only get one frequency per measurement, not the full spectrum.

Periodic signals concentrate their energy in a few frequencies. The peak position tells you the period: a sharp peak at frequency k means the signal repeats every N/k samples. This is the property that quantum phase estimation and Shor's factoring will use to extract a hidden period in polynomial time.

Polylog gates.

A classical FFT computes all N Fourier coefficients in O(N log N) arithmetic operations. The QFT computes the same transform — represented as a quantum state — in O(log² N) gates, that is, O(n²) gates for n = log₂ N qubits. The catch: a quantum measurement returns just one frequency, sampled with probability proportional to its squared magnitude.

Amplitudes that turn.

For the first time in this series, the amplitudes are no longer real. The QFT mixes them through complex roots of unity — phases that rotate around the unit circle. The key formula is short:

QFT |x⟩ = (1/√N) Σy e2πi · xy / N |y⟩
Each computational basis state |x⟩ spreads into a uniform superposition of all states, each weighted by a phase that depends on x · y mod N.

A complex amplitude has two pieces: a magnitude (how much it contributes to the probability) and a phase (where it points in the complex plane). The QFT preserves all magnitudes — every output amplitude has magnitude 1/√N — but rearranges the phases. That's where the information lives.

Below: a single qubit in |0⟩, then in |1⟩. Watch the phase of each output amplitude as you flip between them.

QFT on |0⟩: (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2. Both amplitudes positive real. No phase rotation.

Even on a single qubit, you can see the rotation. QFT|0⟩ is a uniform superposition with both amplitudes positive. QFT|1⟩ is also a uniform superposition — but with the second amplitude rotated 180°, that is, made negative. On more qubits, the rotations become finer. The next section shows the actual circuit.

After the full QFT, every magnitude is exactly 1/√N — the spectrum is "flat" in magnitude. All the structure of the input lives in the phases. That's why a single measurement after the QFT gives a uniformly random outcome by itself; the QFT only becomes useful when combined with another quantum subroutine that biases the measurement toward a particular phase pattern.

A few things to notice in the sandbox: a uniform superposition (all amplitudes equal) becomes the basis state |0…0⟩ after QFT — a perfect peak. A periodic state (only every r-th amplitude non-zero) becomes peaks at multiples of N/r. This is exactly the property quantum phase estimation will exploit to read off eigenvalues, and Shor's factoring will use to find the period of modular exponentiation.

The bridge to Shor.

The QFT on its own gives you nothing — measure a state immediately after applying it and you get a uniformly random outcome. Its power shows up only in composition. Quantum Phase Estimation uses the QFT to read out the eigenphase of a unitary operator. Shor's factoring algorithm uses Phase Estimation, applied to a modular-exponentiation operator, to extract the order of an element modulo N — and from there, factor N.

The lesson: in classical computing, fast Fourier transforms are everywhere precisely because so many problems have hidden periodic structure that becomes visible in the frequency domain. The QFT carries that same property to quantum computing — but with an exponentially shorter circuit. That asymmetry is the entire engine of Shor's exponential speedup over classical factoring.
