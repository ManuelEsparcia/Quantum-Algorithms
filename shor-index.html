Break a number into its primes.

A classical problem with a classical statement. Given a positive integer N, find a non-trivial factor of N. Equivalently: write N = p · q for some p, q > 1. Trivial when N is small — anyone can spot that 15 = 3 · 5. But for the kind of numbers used in modern cryptography, factorization is the problem that keeps internet traffic, banking, and digital signatures safe.

Small example

N = 15 by hand.

Trial division: 15 / 2 = 7.5 (no), 15 / 3 = 5 (yes!). So 15 = 3 · 5. We're done in two steps because 15 is tiny.

Why it matters

RSA cryptography.

RSA picks two large primes p, q and publishes their product N = p · q as the public key. Anyone can encrypt to N, but decrypting requires p and q — which you'd need to factor N to find.

Production RSA uses N of 2048 bits — about 617 decimal digits. The best classical algorithm (GNFS) would take longer than the age of the universe to factor one. Shor's algorithm, on a sufficiently large quantum computer, would factor it in hours.

Shor's algorithm, published in 1994, was the discovery that turned quantum computing from a theoretical curiosity into a strategic priority for governments and intelligence agencies. Nobody has yet built a quantum computer large enough to factor a real RSA key. But the algorithm exists, the math is correct, and the only question is whether the hardware will catch up — and how soon.

The classical state of the art.

Factoring an integer feels like it should be easy — multiplication is fast, so why is its inverse hard? The answer is that classical factoring algorithms have grown more clever over decades, and the best known still runs in sub-exponential time. Not polynomial — but not quite exponential either. Just slow enough that production cryptography is safe, for now.

Method 1 · Trial division

Try every d from 2 to √N. Works for small N, but the cost is O(√N) — exponential in the number of digits. For a 2048-bit key, that's about 2¹⁰²⁴ divisions: more than the number of atoms in the universe.

Method 2 · Quadratic sieve, Pollard's rho

Smarter classical methods. They run in exp(O(√(log N · log log N))) time — sub-exponential but still useless for keys above a few hundred bits.

Method 3 · General Number Field Sieve (GNFS)

The current best classical algorithm. Runs in roughly exp(O(∛(log N · (log log N)²))). The largest RSA modulus factored in public was RSA-250 (829 bits, 2020), using thousands of CPU-years. RSA-2048 is currently out of reach.

From factoring to order-finding.

Shor's first trick isn't quantum — it's a classical reduction. Factoring is hard, but a different problem called order-finding can be reduced to factoring through a chain of number-theoretic identities. So if you can solve order-finding, you can factor. The quantum advantage will live in solving order-finding.

The order of an integer a modulo N is the smallest positive integer r with aʳ ≡ 1 (mod N). Equivalently, the sequence a, a², a³, … taken modulo N repeats with period r. Below, the powers of a = 7 modulo 15 — see if you can spot the period.

Here's the magic. Suppose you've found r, the order of a mod N. If r happens to be even and a^(r/2) ≢ −1 (mod N), then:

aʳ − 1 = (a^(r/2) − 1)(a^(r/2) + 1) ≡ 0 (mod N)
So N divides the product. But N doesn't divide either factor individually (because r is the smallest such exponent, and we ruled out a^(r/2) ≡ −1). That means gcd(a^(r/2) ± 1, N) gives a non-trivial factor of N.

For a = 7, N = 15: 7² = 49 ≡ 4 (mod 15), 7³ = 343 ≡ 13, 7⁴ = 2401 ≡ 1. So r = 4. It's even, and 7² = 4 ≢ −1 ≡ 14. Compute gcd(7² − 1, 15) = gcd(48, 15) = 3. Done. 15 = 3 · 5.

The success probability of "r is usable" is at least 1/2 for typical N, so a few random tries of a almost always succeed. Everything in this reduction is polynomial-time classical. The expensive step — the only expensive step — is finding r.

Why is order-finding hard? Computing aᵏ mod N for any given k is fast (polynomial time via repeated squaring). But finding the period of the sequence requires sampling enough values to spot when it repeats — and the period itself can be exponentially large in the bits of N. Without a clever trick, you'd need exponentially many samples.

Order-finding via phase estimation.

The quantum punch line: there's a unitary operator whose eigenphases are the period information we want, and quantum phase estimation reads eigenphases. The classical reduction handed us a well-defined target; the QFT and QPE machinery from earlier episodes does the heavy lifting; a tiny bit of classical post-processing extracts the answer.

The unitary. Define U_a to act on basis states |y⟩ (where y is an integer between 0 and N − 1) by:

U_a |y⟩ = |a · y mod N⟩
"Multiply by a mod N". Applying U_a repeatedly cycles through the orbit of y under multiplication by a. After r applications you're back where you started: U_aʳ |y⟩ = |aʳ y mod N⟩ = |y⟩.

The eigenvalues. Because U_aʳ = I, every eigenvalue of U_a is an r-th root of unity. They turn out to be exactly e^(2πi · s/r) for s = 0, 1, …, r − 1. So the eigenphases of U_a are multiples of 1/r. Read off any one of them and you've got information about r.

The procedure. Apply QPE to U_a with enough precision bits to resolve 1/r. The measurement returns an integer m such that m/2ᵗ ≈ s/r for some random s. Run the continued fraction expansion of m/2ᵗ to recover r. If unlucky (gcd issue, bad s), try again with a fresh sample.

QPE(U_a) → measure m → m/2ᵗ ≈ s/r → continued fractions → r
The whole quantum part is QPE on a unitary built from modular multiplication. The classical post-processing is continued fractions, a 17th-century technique.

A few things to notice. First, only one step in the entire algorithm is quantum — everything else is elementary number theory you could do on paper. Second, the QPE measurement is the source of randomness: different runs give different s, and not every s is useful. Sometimes you have to repeat. Third, the cost of running the algorithm comes from the modular-exponentiation circuit inside QPE — that's where the gate count for production-sized N reaches the millions.

Two things to watch. First, not every a works — if gcd(a, N) ≠ 1 you've found a factor without needing the quantum part at all; if the order r is odd, you have to try again. Second, more precision bits t in the QPE means a sharper measurement distribution and higher success probability per shot. Production Shor uses t = 2 ⌈log₂ N⌉ for safety.

The algorithm that started everything.

Shor's 1994 paper, "Polynomial-Time Algorithms for Prime Factorization and Discrete Logarithms on a Quantum Computer", landed at exactly the right moment. Public-key cryptography had been operational for two decades. RSA was already protecting financial transactions, classified communications, and the early internet. The realization that a sufficiently large quantum computer could undo all of it — in principle, immediately; in practice, when the hardware arrives — turned quantum computing from a niche academic field into a strategic priority that has driven government investment ever since.

Three decades later, we still haven't factored a number larger than RSA-250 (829 bits, 2020, classical). The largest N factored on real quantum hardware using a faithful implementation of Shor's algorithm is in the dozens, not the hundreds — and the published "factorizations" of larger numbers in the literature are almost all clever cheats that bake in the answer to the circuit somehow. Genuine cryptographically relevant Shor requires millions of logical qubits with deep error correction. Current best estimates put a 2048-bit RSA break at a 20-million-physical-qubit machine running for a day. We're not close yet.

But the algorithm is the algorithm. It's been verified rigorously, implemented (at toy scale) on every flavor of quantum hardware, and stands as the canonical example of an exponential quantum speedup over the best known classical algorithm. It's also the reason "post-quantum cryptography" exists as a field: NIST's standardization of lattice-based, code-based, and hash-based cryptographic schemes is precisely the response to Shor's eventual viability.
